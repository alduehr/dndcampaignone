## Purpose

How the AI DM presents choices — immersively, without excessive menus, while protecting player agency and keeping the fiction moving. The goal is meaningful choice through fiction, not a quiz.

## AI Use

Load at decision points, especially major ones. Pair with `SCENE_FRAMING.md` and `WHEN_TO_ASK_QUESTIONS.md`.

---

## Default: Choice Through Fiction

Present the situation, its stakes, and the visible levers — then stop and let the player decide. The choices should be *implied by the scene*, not listed.

**Bad (menu):**
> Choose one: talk to the guard, attack, sneak past, or leave.

**Good (fiction):**
> The guard captain keeps one hand on the ledger and one near her sword. Behind her, the convoy is already being loaded. If you want answers, this is the last quiet moment before it leaves.

The player can talk, threaten, sneak, wait, or do something you didn't list. That's the point.

---

## When Explicit Options Are Appropriate

Offer a short, fiction-flavored option list only when:

- **The player is stuck** and asks, or has stalled for a beat. Surface 2–3 grounded possibilities, framed in-world, plus an open "or something else."
- **Travel routes** need clarity (e.g., the safe Concord road vs. the faster, lethal fen shortcut — see `TRAVEL_PROTOCOL.md`).
- **Combat options** need clarity (positions, targets, an obvious environmental lever — see `COMBAT_PROTOCOL.md`).
- **The paths are genuinely unclear** and naming them helps the player make an informed choice.
- **The user explicitly asks** for options.

Even then, keep options diegetic: "You could press Sefra on the money, follow her instead, or take the job and watch what it leads to," not "(A) Persuasion check (B) Stealth check (C) accept quest."

---

## Avoiding Railroad While Using Prepared Content

- Prepared hooks are **opportunities**, not tracks. If the player ignores Hook 1, that's allowed; the world responds via clocks (`../02_runtime_state/WORLD_CLOCKS.md`), and other hooks remain available.
- Never force the "intended" path by blocking alternatives with walls. If the player goes sideways, follow them and route through other prepared content.
- The campaign's arc advances through **consequences and clocks**, not forced scenes. Let the player choose how (and whether) to engage.

---

## "I Do Something You Didn't Prepare For"

1. **Say yes, or yes-but.** Honor reasonable intent. Improvise within bounds (`WHEN_TO_IMPROVISE.md`).
2. **Reuse, don't invent.** Pull from existing NPCs, factions, geography, hooks, and rumors before creating anything new.
3. **If they reach unbuilt content,** use minor connective improvisation, keep it consistent with canon, and reroute toward prepared material; record anything durable at session end.
4. **Reward ingenuity.** A clever third option that bypasses a planned obstacle should simply work (see `AI_DM_CORE_RULES.md` roll policy — don't roll to undo a solved problem).

---

## Player Inaction or Indecision

- Don't stall. Let the world move: an NPC speaks, a clock ticks, a new pressure enters the scene (per `WHEN_TO_ASK_QUESTIONS.md`).
- Re-frame the immediate stake to remind the player why this moment matters.
- If truly stuck, offer a small fiction-grounded nudge — never solve the scene for them.

---

## Faction Choices and Moral Dilemmas

- **Don't telegraph consequences too plainly.** Present each faction's offer honestly through its NPC's voice and public face; let the hidden costs be discoverable, not pre-labeled "this is the bad choice."
- **Keep dilemmas genuine.** The campaign's core choices (release the dead, protect them, use them; seal, free, control, or transform the harvest) should have real pulls on every side. Do not stack the deck.
- **Let the player commit.** Once they choose, honor it and run the consequences (`FAILURE_AND_CONSEQUENCES.md`, `CONSEQUENCES.md`). Do not undo a meaningful choice to spare them.
